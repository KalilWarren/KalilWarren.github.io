Research

Research Statement

My research lies at the intersection of psycholinguistics, cognitive neuroscience, and spatial cognition. I investigate how people track, represent, and reason about spatial information during language comprehension — particularly how listeners form and maintain mental models of where sounds and speakers are located in physical space, and how those representations interact with linguistic reference and discourse structure.

I approach these questions using behavioral experiments alongside computational methods. I am also interested in how language processes differ in bilingual speakers, with a focus on French-English bilingualism.

Current Projects

Spatial Memory of Auditory Sources in Language Comprehension

Spatial Cognition Psycholinguistics Auditory Processing Language Comprehension

This project investigates how people encode and retain information about the spatial locations of auditory sources while comprehending spoken language. Using behavioral and neuroimaging paradigms, I examine the degree to which spatial context — specifically where a voice or sound originates in space — is integrated into the mental models listeners construct during discourse processing.

Results were presented at Psychonomics 2025 (Denver, CO) and the Graduate Students in Linguistics Student Research Symposium 2025 (Columbia, SC), in collaboration with Dr. Amit Almor.

Reference Processing Modulation During HD-tDCS

HD-tDCS Reference Processing Cognitive Neuroscience Brain Stimulation

In collaboration with Drs. Scott Wilson, Sara Langley, Dirk-Bart den Ouden, and Amit Almor, this project examined how high-definition transcranial direct current stimulation (HD-tDCS) modulates linguistic reference processing. Findings were presented at the Society for the Neurobiology of Language (2023, Marseille) and Discover USC (2023).

StatTeacher Toolkit

Statistics Education R Shiny Pedagogy Open-Source Tool

Drawing on my experience teaching Psychological Statistics (PSYC 220) as Instructor of Record, the StatTeacher Toolkit is an interactive Python application designed to help statistics instructors and students engage with core statistical concepts through live visualization and simulation.
